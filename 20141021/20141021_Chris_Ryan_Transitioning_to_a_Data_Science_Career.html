Getting a PhD

  • Great things:
    • investigate a topic deeply (relentlessly?) for ~5+ years
    • constant technical communication, typically inter(sub)disciplinary
    • self-direction
  • Differences from the "real world"
    • few deadlines (regarding project completion)
    • can be disconnected from programming "best practices"

Post-PhD

What I still needed to know:

  • Web-related skills

In-person

data science curricula

(my thoughts and advice)

  • please get lots of advice & read blogs (e.g., 1, 2)!
  • bootcamps: full-time & intensive
    • Ones for STEM-PhDs are shorter (Insight DS & Data Incubator)
    • Targeted skill development + a "capstone project"
    • might have mentors from big industry places
  • bootcamps: part time
    • General Assembly
  • Masters degrees from universities
    • mastersindatascience.org
    • e.g., NYU, Columbia

Online

data science curricula

  • Online, mostly free
    • datasciencemasters.org (set of resources compiled by Clare Corthell -- comprehensive in scope)
    • John Hopkins DS "specialization" via Coursera (materials are free, ~$500 for certificate, etc)
  • Online, with fees
    • Berkeley DS masters degree (~$10^4)
    • several others
  • Little-to-no statistics or linear algebra background may be required, but willingness to learn could be important (see respective FAQs & info)

Internships

  • mostly for STEM students, but not always
  • can be found on job posting sites (e.g., LinkedIn)
  • can avoid the financial & time obstacles if available

  • "Doing Data Science" by Cathy O'Neil & Rachel Schutt

    • real-world data science/analysis techniques

    • some experience with programming, basic stats & linear algebra helps, but not required

    • essentially lecture notes processed in book chapters w/ plenty of references

    • very readable, & covers just about all the bases

Personal sabbatical

  • 2 to 3 months to focus on learning
  • Reasons to do one
    • you can list things you'd love to learn, and can envision the steps you'd take to do that
    • you want to do projects & explore
    • life is too busy or distracting to do books/Coursera in free time
    • you have a natural transition time in life
  • Reasons to not do one
    • personal reasons / responsibilities
    • too expensive

Hacker School

  • programming in general                     (not data-specific)
  • ~70 hacker schoolers, ~6 facilitators, several "residents"
  • self-directed learning (no curriculum)
  • project-based
  • free for everyone, with grants available for underrepresented groups in programming
  • job placement help afterward
  • many great things have emerged from HS (including Data Rave)

personal sabbatical

Hacker School

things I did & learned in general

  1. Find a data set you find personally interesting
  2. Work in an iPython Notebook, make some plots     (i.e, exploratory data analysis)
  3. Explore until you notice something unintuitive [optional: apply a technique you just learned about]
  4. Explain your result to a broad audience in a blog post, or at least in a README.md in your GitHub repo

interviews

Interview prep

  • Data scientist interview questions can vary greatly (since positions / company needs vary greatly too)
  • After a phone screen, many good companies will assign a project
    • it's usually fine if you have no experience in this task, but you might have to learn a lot on the spot
  • Consider "120 data science interview questions"
    • compiled by experienced data scientists
    • many have no answers
    • worth $19? Maybe.
  • Be as prepared as possible to analyze business challenges regarding the company's product

interviews

Interview prep

  • Questions I was asked:
    • statistics 101 pop quiz
    • comp sci 101 pop quiz ("Cracking the Code Interview" style)
    • day-to-day business analytics
    • "how would you build a recommendation engine for our product?"
    • "explain something technical to me, of your choosing"
  • Questions I was never asked (but could be common):
    • Hadoop, other data engineering
    • SQL and NoSQL database queries

Leverage your skills and interests

  • Domain expertise is very important for the application of data science
  • If you have a strong background in some area, practice with problems in that domain first (since you understand the trends & challenges)
  • If not, pick an area you find interesting & start reading, learning, & identifying interesting problems

A few more thoughts...

some based on my intuition

  • data science is a still new field
  • younger companies need data engineering before data science
  • show off your projects
    • personal websites are great
    • Github + LinkedIn is a good minimum
  • write blog posts if you have a website
    • explain your projects & analyze results
    • book reviews, course reviews, etc
    • short posts (1 paragraph) are okay
  • you needn't know everything data science-related, now or any time soon
